The problem: your photo library outgrew your phone

Modern iPhones keep growing their camera resolution, 4K video, and ProRes formats faster than base storage tiers grow. A family with a few years of photos and video easily accumulates 500GB–2TB of media, while the phone itself might have 128GB, 256GB, or 512GB of physical storage. Apple's answer is an iCloud+ subscription: pay monthly, forever, to keep everything "available" while your phone only caches a fraction of it locally.

PhotoDump is built around a different answer: move the files to a drive you physically own, once, for a one-time cost — no recurring fee, no dependency on a subscription staying active to keep access to your own memories.

Use Case: 2TB Library, 256GB Phone, 2TB SSD

The exact scenario

Take a common real-world case: an iPhone with 256GB of physical storage, running iCloud Photos with "Optimize iPhone Storage" enabled. Over several years the linked iCloud Photos library has grown to 2TB — full-resolution originals live in iCloud, and the phone only keeps space-saving previews plus whatever was recently viewed, fitting comfortably within its 256GB. The owner buys a 2TB external SSD and wants everything moved there.

Naively, this looks impossible: how can a 256GB device "hold" 2TB of data long enough to copy it anywhere? It cannot, and it does not have to.

How PhotoDump does it without filling the phone

PhotoDump processes the library one file at a time (or a small number of files concurrently, configurable in Settings). For each file, it: (1) requests the original from iCloud if it is not already cached locally, (2) streams the bytes into a small temporary file on the phone — sized to that single file, not the library, (3) copies that temporary file to the external SSD, (4) confirms the copy succeeded, and (5) deletes the temporary file from the phone immediately. The next file then repeats the same cycle.

At any given moment, the phone is only ever holding a handful of in-flight files — typically tens to a few hundred megabytes total, depending on concurrency settings — never the full 2TB library. By the time migration finishes, the phone's local storage usage is back to essentially where it started; the 2TB now lives entirely on the SSD.

Numbers

  • Phone physical storage: 256GB
  • Photo library size (in iCloud): 2TB
  • Destination SSD size: 2TB
  • Peak additional phone storage used during migration: roughly the size of a few in-flight files at once — typically well under 1GB, not 2TB
  • Phone storage used after migration completes: unchanged from before migration started

Use Case: Cancel an Expensive iCloud+ Plan

The math

Apple's 2TB iCloud+ tier is a recurring monthly charge. Paid every month indefinitely, the running total grows without bound — and stops providing access to your own files the moment a payment is missed. A one-time purchase of both PhotoDump and a comparably sized external SSD is a fixed, one-time cost by comparison.

After migrating a library to an owned drive with PhotoDump, many users downgrade their iCloud plan to a smaller (or free) tier, since the drive — not iCloud — is now the long-term home for their media.

Use Case: A Family Sharing One Drive

One SSD, several iPhones

A single external drive can be used by more than one iPhone. Each device is set up with its own destination folder name, so a household with, say, four iPhones can migrate everyone's library onto one shared 4TB or 8TB SSD, each device's files kept separate and never mixed with another device's.

Use Case: A Phone Stuck at "Storage Almost Full"

The everyday trigger

The most common reason people look for a tool like PhotoDump isn't a grand archiving project — it's the "Storage Almost Full" notification blocking a system update or a new app install. Because migration streams file-by-file rather than requiring the whole library to fit on the phone first, PhotoDump can start freeing meaningful, real storage (once files are confirmed migrated and marked safe to delete) within minutes, not after a lengthy pre-processing step.

Use Case: Archiving 4K/ProRes Video Libraries

Video is what actually fills a phone

A minute of 4K video can be several hundred megabytes; ProRes video on newer iPhones is larger still. Video, not photos, is usually what pushes a library from tens of gigabytes into the terabyte range. PhotoDump handles video the same way as photos — streamed, migrated, and verified per file — making it practical to archive years of 4K/ProRes footage onto an external drive without ever needing that footage to fit on the phone at once.

Use Case: Self-Hosting Instead of iCloud or Google Photos

For the privacy- and infrastructure-conscious

PhotoDump's S3-compatible and REST server destinations (currently in Beta) let a technically inclined user migrate their library to infrastructure they run themselves — a home NAS exposing an S3-compatible API, a self-hosted object store, or a personal server — rather than a commercial cloud photo service. No PhotoDump account or server is involved in that transfer at any point.
