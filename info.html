iCloud gives you 5GB free. Photos need more.

A few years of iPhone photos and video easily passes 200GB. iCloud's free tier is 5GB, so almost everyone ends up paying for it — 2TB runs $9.99 a month, forever, with no yearly discount. That's about $120 a year to keep files you already own accessible.

PhotoDump takes the other route: transfer everything to an external drive once, for a one-time cost, and you own the copy outright.

"iPhone storage full" and nothing you delete helps

This is usually what actually sends people looking for a tool like this — not a big archiving project, just the storage warning blocking an update or a new app. Apple's own Photos app can export originals one at a time ("Export Unmodified Original"), but doing that for a few thousand photos isn't realistic.

PhotoDump automates the same idea: it transfers files one by one and only marks each one safe to delete after confirming it landed on the drive. Space starts freeing up within minutes, not after some long prep step.

Moving a 2TB library off a 256GB phone

Here's the case that sounds impossible: an iPhone with 256GB of storage, iCloud Photos set to "Optimize iPhone Storage," and a library that's grown to 2TB in iCloud over the years. The phone only ever held previews and recently viewed files — the originals live in iCloud. Buy a 2TB SSD, and it looks like the phone would need to somehow "hold" 2TB to copy it anywhere.

It doesn't. PhotoDump downloads and transfers one file at a time, so at any moment the phone is only holding whatever's currently in transit — typically under a gigabyte, not the whole library. By the time it's done, the phone's storage is right back where it started, and the SSD has everything.

Cancelling a $9.99/month iCloud+ plan

A 2TB iCloud+ subscription costs $9.99 every month with no end date — miss a payment and you lose access to your own files. A one-time SSD plus a one-time app purchase is a fixed cost by comparison. Most people who migrate their library to their own drive downgrade iCloud to the free tier afterward, since the drive is now where their photos actually live.

One drive, one family, several iPhones

A single external drive isn't limited to one phone. Set up each device with its own folder name and a family can put everyone's library — parents, kids, whoever — on one shared 4TB or 8TB drive, with each person's files kept separate automatically.

Video is the real space hog

A minute of 4K video runs several hundred megabytes; ProRes on newer iPhones is bigger still. Video, not photos, is usually what pushes a library from tens of gigabytes into the terabytes. PhotoDump moves video the same way it moves photos — one file at a time, verified after each transfer — so years of 4K or ProRes footage can move to a drive without ever needing to fit on the phone.

Running your own storage instead of a cloud provider

PhotoDump's S3-compatible and REST server support (Beta) works with a home NAS, a self-hosted object store, or any server you control — not just USB drives. Nothing passes through PhotoDump's own servers, because there aren't any.
